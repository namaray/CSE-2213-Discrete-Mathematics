1. What is a Proposition?

Definition: A proposition is a declarative sentence that can be either true or false, but cannot be both, and cannot be neither.

✅ Examples of Propositions

  • "The sun rises in the east" (True proposition)
  • "2 plus 2 equals 5" (False proposition)
  • x + 1 = y, given that x = 5 and y = 6 (Equation with values)

❌ NOT Propositions

  • "What is your name?" (Question)
  • "Tell me your name." (Order/Command)
  • x + 1 = y (Equation without assigned values; neither true nor false)
The Liar Paradox: "I am lying."
If it is true, you are lying (making it false). If it is false, you are telling the truth (making it true). Because it is both true and false simultaneously, it is not a proposition.

2. Variables & Proposition Types

We use variables (like p, q, a, b) to represent propositions to avoid writing out full sentences.

  • p = "The earth is round"
  • q = "Argentina won the world cup"

Atomic vs. Compound Propositions

  • Atomic Propositions: Cannot be broken down further. (e.g., "I live in Dhaka")
  • Compound Propositions: Formed by combining two or more atomic propositions using logical operators. (e.g., "I live in Dhaka and I am a lecturer")

4. Conditional Statements (Implies)

Denoted by p → q. Means "If p, then q".

  • p: Premise / Hypothesis
  • q: Conclusion / Consequence

Example: "If it rains (p), I will stay home (q)."

When is this false? The only time a conditional statement is false is if the hypothesis happens, but the conclusion does not (i.e., It rains, but you go out anyway).

Sufficient & Necessary Conditions

For p → q:

  • p is a sufficient condition for q. (Raining is sufficient reason for me to stay home).
  • q is a necessary condition for p. (In order to pass, it is necessary that you study).

14 Ways to Express p → q

  1. if p, then q
  2. if p, q
  3. p implies q
  4. q if p
  5. q when p
  6. q whenever p
  7. q follows from p
  8. q provided that p
  9. q unless ¬p
  10. p only if q
  11. p is sufficient for q
  12. a sufficient condition for q is p
  13. q is necessary for p
  14. a necessary condition for p is q

5. Biconditional Statements (If and Only If)

Denoted by p ↔ q. Means "p if and only if q".

6. Converse, Contrapositive, and Inverse

From the conditional statement p → q ("If I am in Dhaka then I am in Bangladesh"), we can form three new related conditional statements:

  • Converse: q → p
    If I am in Bangladesh, then I am in Dhaka.
  • Inverse: ¬p → ¬q
    If I am not in Dhaka then I am not in Bangladesh.
  • Contrapositive: ¬q → ¬p
    If I am not in Bangladesh, then I am not in Dhaka.
Logical Equivalence: A conditional statement and its contrapositive are logically equivalent.
p → q ≡ ¬q → ¬p

7. Precedence of Logical Operators

There may be several logical operators in a sentence. We keep the precedence in mind while translating as a strategy:

8. Translating Sentences into Propositions

Example 1: "You can graduate only if you have completed the requirements of your major and you do not owe money to the university and you do not have an overdue library book."

Let:
g : "You can graduate"
r : "You have completed the requirements of your major"
m : "You owe money to the university"
o : "You have an overdue library book"

Translation: g → (r ∧ ¬m ∧ ¬o)
Example 2: "To use the wireless network in the airport you must pay the daily fee unless you are a subscriber to the service."

Let:
w : "You use the wireless network in the airport"
p : "You must pay the daily fee"
s : "You are a subscriber to the service"

Translation: ¬s → (w → p)
