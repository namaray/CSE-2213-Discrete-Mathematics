1. Product Rule & Sum Rule

The Product Rule

If a procedure can be broken down into a sequence of two tasks, where the first task can be done in n1 ways and the second task can be done in n2 ways, then there are n1 × n2 ways to do the procedure.

Example 1: Getting Dressed
Choose 1 shirt (from 3), 1 pants (from 2), 1 shoes (from 2).
Total: 3 × 2 × 2 = 12 outfits.
Example 2: Password Creation
3 characters: 1 uppercase letter, 1 digit, 1 special symbol (from 5).
Total: 26 × 10 × 5 = 1300 passwords.
Example 3: Assigning Offices
Assigning 12 offices to Sonia and Patel.
Total: 12 choices for Sonia × 11 choices for Patel = 132 ways.
Example 4: Bit Strings
How many bit strings of length 7?
Total: 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 27 = 128.
Example 5: License Plates
3 letters followed by 3 digits.
Total: 26 × 26 × 26 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 17,576,000.

The Sum Rule

If a task can be done either in one of n1 ways or in one of n2 ways (where the options are mutually exclusive), then there are n1 + n2 ways to do the task.

Example 6: Committee Representative
Choose either a math faculty (37) or a math major (83).
Total: 37 + 83 = 120 choices.
Example 7: UIU FYDP Projects
Dr. Sarwar offers 4, Dr. Farid offers 5, Dr. Shatabda offers 7.
Total: 4 + 5 + 7 = 16 projects.
Example 8: Dhaka to Barishal
Direct Bus (8), Broken Bus route (5 × 7 = 35), Launch (8).
Total: 8 + 35 + 8 = 51 ways.

2. The Pigeonhole Principle

If N objects are placed into k boxes, then there is at least one box containing at least ⌈N/k⌉ objects.

Example 9: Birth Months
Among 100 people, how many are guaranteed to share a birth month?
Ans: ⌈100 / 12⌉ = ⌈8.33⌉ = 9 people.
Example 10: Exam Scores
How many students must be in a class to guarantee 2 students get the same score (0 to 100)?
Ans: Possible scores = 101. We need 101 + 1 = 102 students.
Example 11: Drawing Socks in the Dark
Drawer has 12 brown and 12 black socks.
- To guarantee a matching pair: 3 socks.
- To guarantee two black socks: Pick all 12 brown + 2 black = 14 socks.
Example 12: Mobile Numbers in Bangladesh
Format `01S-XXXXXXXX` (8 digits). 108 = 100 million unique numbers per operator.
If 180 million people need phones, how many operators (S) are needed?
Ans: ⌈180,000,000 / 100,000,000⌉ = ⌈1.8⌉ = 2 operators.

3. Permutations & Combinations

Permutations (Order Matters)

P(n, r) = n! / (n - r)!

Example 13: Arranging Letters
How many permutations of ABCDEFGH contain the string "ABC"?
Sol: Treat "ABC" as 1 block. Total items = 6 [ABC, D, E, F, G, H]. P(6,6) = 6! = 720.

How many contain A, B, C together in any order?
Sol: 6! (for the blocks) × 3! (internal arrangement of ABC) = 720 × 6 = 4320.

How many do NOT contain A, B, C together?
Sol: Total permutations (8!) - Permutations with ABC together (4320) = 40320 - 4320 = 36000.

How many have A, B, C completely separated?
Sol: Arrange the 5 other letters (5! = 120). This creates 6 gaps. Place A, B, C in the 6 gaps: P(6,3) = 120. Total = 120 × 120 = 14400.
Example 14: Rearranging SILHOUETTE
10 letters (T=2, E=2).
- No special condition: 10! / (2! × 2!) = 907,200.
- All vowels together (I,O,U,E,E): Treat as 1 block. 6 blocks total. (6! / 2!) × (5! / 2!) = 360 × 60 = 21,600.

Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter)

C(n, r) = n! / (r! × (n - r)!)

Example 15: Forming a Committee
9 Math faculty, 11 CS faculty. Form a committee of 3 Math and 4 CS.
Sol: C(9, 3) × C(11, 4) = 84 × 330 = 27,720 ways.
Example 16: Faculty Selection with Restriction
7 women, 9 men. Select a committee of 5 with at least one woman.
Sol: Total unrestricted committees = C(16, 5) = 4368.
Committees with NO women (only men) = C(9, 5) = 126.
At least one woman = 4368 - 126 = 4242 ways.
